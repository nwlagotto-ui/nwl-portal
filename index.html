Setting Up the Space

The crate and exercise pen work together as a unit. Connect the pen to the crate opening so the puppy has a larger safe area when you cannot directly supervise. The crate is for sleeping and resting; the pen is for when you need them contained but want them to have more room than the crate alone provides.

Put the crate in your bedroom for the first few weeks — at least. Your presence is genuinely settling to a puppy who has just left their litter, and the middle-of-the-night bathroom trips are much more manageable when you are right there.

Cover the crate at night with a light blanket on three sides if you want — this makes it feel more den-like. Leave the door side open for airflow. A covered crate tends to produce calmer, quieter settling behavior.

Puppy-Proofing

Do this before the puppy arrives, not after the first incident. Get down to puppy level and look around each room they will have access to. The most common hazards:

High Priority

Electrical cords — Run them behind furniture or use cord protectors. Puppies chew anything that dangles, and the consequence of chewing a live cord is severe.

Drapery and blind cords — Tie these up out of reach. Strangulation risk.

Medications, vitamins, supplements — All of them. Many human medications are acutely toxic to dogs at small doses. Cabinet latches if you have low cabinets.

Toxic plants — We have sent you a full list. Sago palms are acutely lethal. When in doubt, move it out of reach or remove it entirely.

Also Important

Trash cans with lids (or inside cabinets), cleaning products stored low, laundry detergent pods (highly toxic), shoes left on the floor, children's toys with small parts. Baby gates on all stairs — growth plate damage from repeated stair use is real and cumulative.

This section covers the hazards that matter most in the first weeks — what to watch for, what your vet needs to know, and what to do if something goes wrong. Most of it is simple once you know it.

Scheduling Your First Vet Visit

Your health guarantee requires a vet visit within three days of pickup. Book it now — before you pick up — so you are not scrambling to find an opening when the puppy is already home. Call ahead and let them know you are bringing in a new puppy so they can plan for the full intake exam.

Bring everything from your pickup packet: the health certificate, the full vaccination record, deworming history, and the parent health testing results. Your vet will want to see the vaccination schedule to know when boosters are due. Also bring a fresh fecal sample — young puppies commonly carry Giardia or Coccidia even when they look perfectly healthy, and catching it at this first visit is far better than missing it.

At this visit, ask your vet about local parasite risks: heartworm protocol for your area, flea and tick prevention, and leptospirosis vaccination if it is recommended in your region. These vary significantly by geography.

One thing worth mentioning directly: let your vet know this is a Lagotto Romagnolo. The breed has a few specific health considerations — including some medication sensitivities — that are worth flagging. A vet who has never seen a Lagotto before is not a problem; a vet who has that context will simply be better prepared.

Toxic Foods

None of the following, ever, in any amount:

Chocolate — all forms. Dark and baking chocolate are most dangerous but milk chocolate in sufficient quantity is also toxic. Theobromine accumulates and causes cardiac and neurological effects.

Grapes and raisins — the toxic compound is unidentified, which means there is no safe dose. Acute kidney failure can occur even from small amounts in some dogs.

Xylitol — a sweetener found in sugar-free gum, many peanut butters, some yogurts, and hundreds of other products. Check labels before giving any human food. Causes rapid, severe hypoglycemia.

Onions and garlic — in any form, including powder. Destroys red blood cells. Cumulative effect means repeated small exposures are dangerous, not just a single large one.

Macadamia nuts — neurological effects including weakness and tremors. Mechanism unknown.

Alcohol, caffeine, moldy food — all toxic. Keep the trash secured.

Pet Poison Helpline: 1-855-764-7661 — available 24/7. Save this in your phone now, before you need it.

Toxic Plants

The attached PDF in your pickup packet covers the full list. The one that warrants special mention is the sago palm — all parts of this plant are acutely lethal to dogs, including the seeds and roots. If you have one, remove it entirely. No exceptions.

When in doubt about any plant: move it out of reach. If the puppy cannot reach it, it cannot harm them. For plants you want to keep at floor level, look them up before the puppy comes home rather than after the first incident.

Joint Health in the First Year

The growth plates in a puppy's bones do not fully close until 12 to 18 months. Before then, repetitive high-impact activity can cause damage that shows up not now but years later as joint problems. The rules for the first year are straightforward:

No running on leash, no jogging, no cycling. No repetitive stair climbing — use baby gates and carry the puppy when needed. No jumping from heights: off couches, out of cars, over obstacles. No high-intensity fetch sessions with hard cuts and launches. Free play at the puppy's own pace in a yard is fine; sustained, handler-driven impact is not.

Keep the puppy lean throughout the first year. Extra weight on developing joints is a meaningful, well-documented risk factor. If you can feel the ribs easily without pressing, the puppy is at a good weight.

Water, Medications, and Other Hazards

Toilet lids down — a young puppy can fall in and drown. This sounds extreme; it is not.

Laundry pods — acutely toxic and visually appealing to puppies. Keep them locked away, not just on a high shelf.

Cleaning products — anything under the kitchen or bathroom sink. Cabinet latches are cheap and worth it.

Children and other pets — supervise all interactions in the first weeks. A child who does not yet understand dog body language and a puppy who does not yet understand how to ask for space is a combination that needs adult management, not optimism.

The weeks between now and pickup are, from a developmental standpoint, among the most consequential of your dog's entire life. Understanding why — and what to do with that knowledge — is one of the most useful things this section can give you.

The Socialization Window

Between roughly 8 and 16 weeks of age, a puppy's brain is in a unique developmental state. During this period, the nervous system is actively building its model of what is normal — what things, people, sounds, and surfaces belong in the world. Experiences during this window are processed differently than they will be at any other time: they are encoded as baseline, as expected, as safe.

After approximately 16 weeks, the brain shifts from an open, curious orientation to a more cautious one. Novel things become suspect rather than interesting. This is not a problem — it is adaptive — but it means that the foundation built during the open window is genuinely difficult to retrofit later. Under-socialized puppies become anxious adults, and anxiety in dogs creates a cascade of behavioral problems that are hard to resolve.

The American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior recommends that socialization begin before the vaccine series is complete. The disease risk from thoughtful early socialization in appropriate settings is generally lower than the behavioral risk of keeping a puppy isolated until 16 weeks. Your vet will guide you on which environments are appropriate before full vaccination — puppy kindergarten classes with vaccination requirements, the homes of vaccinated dogs, and surfaces that have not been contaminated by unvaccinated animals are all generally considered safe.

What to Socialize To

Aim for quality and variety across all of these categories before 16 weeks:

People

Men with beards, children of different ages, people in hats or sunglasses, people using mobility aids or moving unpredictably, people in uniforms. The goal is variety, not quantity — ten genuinely different types of people is more valuable than fifty similar ones.

Sounds

Traffic, thunder recordings, appliances running, the vacuum cleaner, the doorbell, children playing, a crowd. Play recordings at low volume during meals initially — the pairing with something pleasant changes how the sound is processed.

Surfaces and Environments

Grass, gravel, tile, metal grates, sand, carpet, hardwood. Different outdoor environments — a park, a parking lot, a hardware store that allows dogs. Novel environments are inherently enriching for this breed.

Handling

Paws, ears, mouth, being held by strangers, being picked up from behind, having their collar held. Do this gently and with treats throughout, starting on day one. You are building the tolerance your groomer and vet will depend on for the next fifteen years.

Foundation Training: What to Start First

Do not wait until the puppy is "settled in" to begin training — the first week home is the ideal time to start, because patterns established early are the ones that stick. Keep sessions to three to five minutes maximum. Always end on a success. Lagottos learn fast and fatigue fast.

Name Recognition

Say the name once in a warm, upbeat tone. The moment the puppy looks at you, mark it (click or say "yes") and reward. Do this twenty times in the first day. Within 48 hours most Lagotto puppies are reliably orienting to their name.

Recall — The Most Important Command

Coming when called should be the most rewarding thing that happens in your puppy's day — every single time, without exception. Never call the puppy to you for something unpleasant (nail trim, bath, going in the crate when they do not want to). Go get them for those things instead. If you poison the recall word by using it for things they dislike, you will spend years trying to rebuild it.

Sit

Hold a treat near the nose and move it slowly back over the head. The puppy's rear goes down as the nose goes up. Mark and reward the moment the bottom touches the ground. Once the behavior is reliable, name it: say "sit" just before the motion. This takes two or three sessions.

Crate Introduction

Toss treats into the crate and let the puppy go in and come back out freely. Do not close the door for the first few sessions. Build a strong positive association before confinement is ever introduced. This investment pays off every single night for years.

Separation Anxiety Prevention

This is one of the areas where early intervention matters most and where most people wait too long. A Lagotto who develops true separation anxiety is a genuinely difficult problem — it causes real distress for the dog and significant disruption for the household. The good news is that the prevention protocol is simple if you start it in the first week.

Start gentle separations immediately: put the puppy in the crate while you are still home, leave the room for thirty seconds, come back calmly. Build gradually to longer durations over days. The goal is teaching the puppy that your departure is not an event — it is just a normal part of life that always ends with your return.

Do not make departures and arrivals into emotional moments. No dramatic goodbyes, no excited greetings when you return. Calm, matter-of-fact. The puppy takes their emotional cues from you.

Use a camera to monitor the puppy when you first leave them alone — what you see on camera is the truth, not what you assume based on silence when you return. A puppy who is silently pacing or drooling is not "fine." A puppy who settles and sleeps is fine. The attached article from the Whole Dog Journal covers the distinction between true separation anxiety, normal adjustment, and boredom in detail — it is worth reading before you assume the worst or dismiss the signs.

The Lagotto coat is one of the most distinctive and most misunderstood things about the breed. Understanding how it actually works will save you significant money, prevent real problems, and let you have an informed conversation with any groomer you work with.

What Makes the Lagotto Coat Different

The Lagotto has a dense, woolly double coat made of hair rather than fur. The distinction matters: hair grows continuously and does not shed the way fur does. Instead, loose hairs stay trapped within the curls. If those trapped hairs are not regularly combed out, they compress against each other and against the skin — a process called felting, not matting. The difference is significant: a mat can be worked apart; a felt is a compressed, solid mass that has to be clipped out. It can conceal skin issues underneath and is genuinely uncomfortable for the dog.

The other thing to understand is that most US groomers have never worked on a Lagotto. They will default to what they know — typically a poodle clip, which is incorrect for this breed both aesthetically and functionally. We will cover exactly what to tell your groomer below.

The Coat Transition Warning

Between approximately 9 and 12 months, your puppy's soft puppy coat begins to give way to the denser, woolier adult coat. This transition period is when felting is most likely to occur — the two coat textures intertwine and compress if not managed carefully. This is not the time to let grooming slide. We will send you a heads-up when your puppy is approaching this stage, but keep it in the back of your mind from the beginning.

The Critical Rules

Never brush a dry coat. Brushing dry curls disrupts the curl structure and causes the very tangling you are trying to prevent. Combing dry is fine; brushing is not.

Never use conditioner. Conditioner causes severe felting in the Lagotto coat. If a groomer has used conditioner and you need to strip it, a clarifying or stripping shampoo is the only option. Warn any groomer explicitly about this before they start.

Comb before bathing, not after. Bathing a coat with any tangles already in it sets those tangles permanently as the coat dries.

Air dry only. Blow drying flattens the curl structure and produces the appearance of a poodle coat. If a groomer wants to blow dry for finishing, ask them not to. The coat needs to dry naturally to maintain its texture.

Starting Grooming From Day One

You do not need to know how to groom a Lagotto to start grooming preparation from day one. What you are building in the first weeks is not technique — it is tolerance. A puppy who grows up comfortable with the table, the comb, having its paws handled, its ears touched, its face worked around — that puppy becomes an adult dog who stands quietly for grooming. That tolerance is one of the most valuable things you can give them, and the window to build it with minimal effort is now.

Short sessions, positive, treat-rewarded. Touch the paws. Look in the ears. Run a comb gently through the coat. Pick up each foot. Open the mouth. None of this needs to be a full grooming session — two minutes at a time, several times a week, is all it takes.

Your puppy's nails have been trimmed regularly since birth. Continue this — overgrown nails change gait mechanics and cause long-term structural problems. A Dremel rotary tool is gentler and faster once the puppy is comfortable with the vibration; start by simply turning it on near them during meals so it becomes background noise before it is ever introduced to a nail.

The Felt-Prone Areas

These are the spots that need more frequent attention than the rest of the body: under the ears, the collar area (remove the collar regularly and comb beneath it), the armpits, and the groin. Check these areas more frequently than the rest of the coat, especially during the coat transition period.

What to Tell Your Groomer

Bring this to the appointment if it helps. The key points for a groomer who has not worked on a Lagotto:

Body and legs: scissors to 1 to 2 centimeters, following the natural body lines. This is not a clipper breed.

Head: rounded and curly, 3 to 4 centimeters at the crown. The ears should be short at the top and slightly longer toward the base — the reverse of a poodle ear. Do not cut the hair between the eyes. Trim eyebrows only enough to maintain visibility.

No blow drying for finishing. Air dry only.

No conditioner. Under any circumstances.

We have included the Laura Tanos cut guide from Regun Kennels in your pickup packet — this is the clearest visual reference available in the US for the correct Lagotto trim and is worth sharing directly with your groomer.

Ear Care

Hair grows inside Lagotto ear canals, and their drop ears limit airflow — two factors that combine to create a structural predisposition to ear infections if ears are not managed proactively. Check the ears weekly. Trim the hair at the ear canal opening to improve airflow. Dry thoroughly after any water exposure — Lagottos love water, and a wet ear canal left undried is a recurring problem in this breed.

Current veterinary consensus has moved away from routine ear plucking. Plucking can cause microtrauma to the canal lining that increases infection risk. Trim at the opening; pluck only if your vet specifically recommends it for your dog's anatomy.

Signs of infection: head shaking, pawing at the ear, odor, redness, discharge, or sensitivity when the ear is touched. Do not treat with OTC solutions — many are ineffective, and some can cause damage if the eardrum is compromised. Have the vet look.

Dental Care

Puppies teethe between approximately 3 and 6 months. During this period they chew more, may drool slightly, and may show mild gum redness — all normal. Provide appropriate chew outlets: bully sticks, yak chews, frozen Kongs, rubber toys designed for teething. Redirect chewing from inappropriate objects to appropriate ones consistently.

Build mouth-touching tolerance with treats first, long before you introduce a toothbrush. Once the puppy accepts your finger in their mouth comfortably, move to a finger brush with dog-safe toothpaste — never human toothpaste, which contains xylitol and fluoride. Brushing three times a week significantly reduces dental disease risk over a lifetime. Daily is better.

See your vet if you notice excessive drooling beyond normal teething, persistent bad breath at any age, reluctance to eat, or bleeding gums that does not resolve quickly.

There is a piece of information about Lagottos that, once you understand it, changes how you think about exercise, enrichment, and what this breed actually needs. It is about the nose — and it is more significant than most owners realize going in.

Exercise: Less Than You Might Think

A common assumption about working breeds is that they need enormous amounts of physical exercise. With Lagottos, this is not quite right. A healthy adult Lagotto does well with 30 to 60 minutes of intentional daily exercise. What they need alongside that — and what most people underestimate — is mental engagement.

For your puppy specifically, the growth plate rules covered in the safety section apply: keep walks short, avoid high-impact activity, no running or jumping from heights. Five minutes per month of age, twice a day, is a useful guideline for the first several months. A three-month-old puppy needs about fifteen minutes of walk, not an hour.

Young puppies also need far more sleep than most people expect — sixteen to eighteen hours a day. An overtired, overstimulated puppy bites harder, loses patience faster, and struggles to learn. Enforcing nap times in the crate is not cruelty; it is essential to their development and to your sanity.

Mental Stimulation: What Actually Tires This Breed

A mentally tired Lagotto is a calmer, more settled, more manageable dog than a physically tired one. The breed was selected over centuries to work with its brain — problem-solving, scenting, making independent decisions in the field. That cognitive drive does not disappear because the dog lives in a house. It needs somewhere to go.

When Lagottos are bored, they are creative about it. Digging, barking, chewing, redecorating — these are not bad behavior. They are a working dog inventing its own job. Your goal is to give them a better one first.

What works especially well: frozen Kongs (stuff with peanut butter, wet food, or kibble mixed with broth, freeze overnight — occupies a puppy meaningfully), snuffle mats (scatter kibble across the mat for foraging — tiring in a way a bowl is not), puzzle feeders at graduated difficulty levels, flirt poles for short high-intensity play sessions, and simply hiding in another room and calling the puppy — hide and seek doubles as recall training.

The Nose

The Lagotto Romagnolo has one of the most refined scenting abilities of any breed in the world. This is not an accident — it is the product of centuries of selection for a single highly specific task: finding truffles buried beneath the soil of Italian forests, by smell alone, through layers of earth and competing organic material. A human nose has approximately 5 million olfactory receptors. The Lagotto's sits at the high end of the canine range, somewhere around 300 million.

This is the nose your puppy is bringing into your home. The practical consequence is that scent-based enrichment is the highest-value mental exercise you can give a Lagotto. Five minutes of nose work is more tiring and more satisfying to this dog than thirty minutes on leash. It engages them neurologically in a way that running simply does not.

Starting Nose Work at Home — Today

You do not need equipment, training classes, or truffles to begin. Here is the progression starting from day one:

Week one: Hold a treat in your closed fist. Let the puppy sniff and work at your hand. The moment their nose touches your fist, open it. Repeat ten times. You have introduced the concept that the nose finds the reward.

Week two: Place three overturned plastic cups on the floor. Put a treat under one while the puppy watches. Release them to sniff. When they indicate the right cup, lift it and reward. Most Lagotto puppies understand this game within one session.

Week three onward: Hide treats in a room while the puppy waits outside. Release them with a cue — "find it" — and let them search. Start with obvious hides, progress to genuinely difficult ones. This is the backbone of nose work and Lagottos take to it with a speed that is often startling.

The AKC Scent Work program offers a structured path forward from here, with titling opportunities at all levels and clubs in most areas. It is one of the few activities that gives a Lagotto a genuine outlet for what they were built to do. Worth looking into once the puppy is settled.

A Note on Truffle Hunting

If the idea of actually hunting truffles interests you — and the Pacific Northwest is genuinely one of the finest truffle environments in North America, with Oregon white and black truffles growing wild — your Lagotto is the right tool for the job. The foundation you build through nose work at home transfers directly to truffle work. The Oregon Truffle Festival and regional truffle hunting organizations are good starting points. We will have more on this when the time is right.

This section covers the health conditions that are specific to the Lagotto Romagnolo — what they are, what responsible breeding does about them, and what you should know going into your first vet visit. The intent is not to alarm you; it is to make you an informed owner who asks the right questions and recognizes the right signs.

All Northwest Lagotto breeding dogs are tested for the conditions below. You will receive the specific parent test results in your pickup packet.

Lagotto Storage Disease (LSD)

LSD is a progressive neurological condition caused by a mutation in the ATG4D gene. It disrupts the normal cellular process for breaking down and recycling proteins. Affected dogs typically begin showing symptoms between four months and four years: increasing anxiety, changes in gait and coordination, and eventually significant neurological deterioration. It is serious and does not improve over time.

The genetic test for LSD is definitive. The condition is autosomal recessive — both parents must carry the mutation to produce an affected puppy. A breeder who tests and does not breed two carriers together cannot produce an affected puppy. This is straightforward to prevent; the only way it happens is through careless or untested breeding.

If you ever notice progressive changes in your Lagotto's coordination, anxiety levels, or gait — particularly in the first few years — mention LSD specifically to your vet and ask for a neurological evaluation.

Benign Familial Juvenile Epilepsy (BFJE)

BFJE is a neurological condition unique to the Lagotto breed, caused by a mutation in the LGI2 gene. The word "benign" in the name is accurate and important: affected puppies develop focal seizures between approximately five and thirteen weeks of age — tremors, brief episodes of uncoordinated movement, moments of apparent stiffness — and then the seizures resolve on their own, typically by the time the puppy is three to four months old.

If your puppy shows any of these signs in the first weeks home, contact us and your vet. Knowing about BFJE means you will recognize what you are looking at rather than being frightened by something that is, in fact, self-limiting. Like LSD, it is autosomal recessive and fully preventable through testing. Parent results will be in your packet.

Joint Health

Lagottos are not a breed with dramatically elevated hip dysplasia rates, but joint health is worth managing proactively in any active medium-sized dog. The exercise guidelines in Module V cover the growth plate rules. The additional things worth knowing:

Keep the puppy lean through the first year — extra weight on developing joints is well-documented as a risk factor. Around six months, consider starting a joint supplement: glucosamine with green-lipped mussel has reasonable evidence behind it as a preventive measure and very low risk. YuMOVE is the product I use and recommend. We will revisit this at the six-month post-pickup milestone.

If your puppy is ever reluctant to bear weight on a limb, noticeably stiff after rest, or seems sore after activity — get it evaluated. Do not assume it will resolve on its own.

Eye Health

Lagottos have a moderate prevalence of hereditary eye conditions including cataracts, corneal dystrophy, and retinal dysplasia. Breeding dogs should be examined by a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist. Ask your vet to take a baseline look at your puppy's eyes at the first visit. If you notice cloudiness, persistent discharge, squinting, or any change in your dog's vision as they age, a referral to a veterinary ophthalmologist is appropriate.

Feeding Your Lagotto Puppy

Your puppy is eating Royal Canin Medium Puppy, three times daily. At pickup, feeding will be approximately one-third cup per meal — one cup total per day. Adjust as the puppy grows using body condition rather than the calendar: you should be able to feel the ribs easily without pressing, and the puppy should have a visible waist when viewed from above.

Keep the same food for at least the first week, ideally two. A new home is already significant stress; adding a food transition produces digestive upset reliably. If you want to change foods, do it gradually over 7 to 10 days: 25% new food for three days, then half and half, then 75% new, then complete.

Feed at consistent times each day. A predictable feeding schedule produces predictable digestion, which makes housetraining dramatically easier. Make the last meal no later than 5 or 6pm to allow time for a final bathroom trip before bed.

Stay on puppy food until approximately 12 months. Medium-breed puppy formulas are calibrated for the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio that growing joints require — switching to adult food too early can interfere with bone development. At 12 months, transition gradually to an adult formula using the same process above.

If loose stools occur in the first few days, add a teaspoon of plain canned pumpkin (not pumpkin pie filling) to each meal. If stools are soft for more than a day or two, or if you see any blood, get a fecal sample to your vet promptly.

This is the section I enjoy most. Not a checklist, not a safety guide — just the story of the dog you are about to bring home. I have been working with this breed for years and I still find their history remarkable. Knowing where a breed comes from helps you understand who they are as individuals.

An Ancient Italian Working Dog

The Lagotto Romagnolo is one of the oldest dog breeds in the world, with documented history stretching back to at least the late 15th century in the Emilia-Romagna region of northern Italy. The name tells the story: "Lagotto" comes from the regional dialect word for the marshy lagoons around Ravenna, and "Romagnolo" refers to the region itself. They were water dogs — bred to retrieve waterfowl from the cold wetlands, their dense woolly coats protecting them from the water and the thorny undergrowth.

For centuries this was their life: working alongside hunters in the marshes, doing the hard physical labor that the Italian countryside required. They were not a nobleman's dog — they were a working man's dog, valued for toughness, intelligence, and an extraordinary nose.

The Truffle

In the late 19th century, much of the Romagna marshland was drained for agriculture. The waterfowl were gone. The Lagotto's original work had vanished.

But something else had been quietly discovered. The same nose that had been trained for centuries to find birds in water could find something far more valuable buried underground. Truffles — intensely aromatic fungi that grow symbiotically with the roots of certain trees — were among the most sought-after culinary ingredients in Italy, and among the hardest to find. Truffle hunters had long used pigs, which locate truffles by smell because the fungi produce a compound that mimics a porcine pheromone. But pigs are difficult to transport, tend to eat what they find, and are impossible to walk discreetly through a village.

Dogs were better. And of all the dogs available, the Lagotto was the best. Their nose was already exceptional. They were small enough to move through forest undergrowth, athletic enough to work for hours, and trainable enough to learn to dig up what they found and hand it over rather than consume it. Over several generations, this became the breed's defining work — and selective breeding reinforced it further.

Today the Lagotto Romagnolo is the only purebred dog in the world officially recognized as a truffle-hunting breed. It is their designated role in the FCI breed standard. In Italy they are a national treasure — as intertwined with the culture of truffles as the landscape itself.

The Nose in Numbers

A human nose has approximately 5 million scent receptors. A dog's nose, on average, has between 220 and 300 million. The Lagotto's olfactory ability — honed by centuries of selection pressure for scent work in challenging outdoor conditions — sits at the high end of that range. They can detect truffle scent through several inches of soil, beneath layers of leaves and competing organic material, in rain, wind, and cold.

This is the nose your puppy is bringing into your home. It will find the one piece of food that fell behind the refrigerator three months ago. It will know before you do when you are upset. It will detect that a stranger has been in your house. Treat this instrument with respect — and give it work to do.

They Almost Disappeared

By the 1970s, the Lagotto Romagnolo had dwindled to near-extinction. Industrialized agriculture, declining rural traditions, and the erosion of the truffle-hunting culture had removed the context in which these dogs had thrived for centuries. There were only a handful of dedicated breeders keeping the bloodlines alive.

In the late 1980s, a group of passionate Italian breeders organized. They standardized the breed, established a club, registered with the FCI, and began a deliberate preservation effort. The AKC recognized the breed in 2015. The dog you are getting is the direct result of that rescue — a breed pulled back from the edge by people who understood what was at stake.

Who Is This Dog at Home
